# Light Types and Scene Lighting Teacher Notes

## Purpose of the Lesson

Lighting tools are creative choices. The type of light, not just its brightness, changes where the viewer looks and how the scene feels. This lesson gives students a practical production task connected to the transcript range 01:32:47-01:41:19.

## Teacher Mini-Lesson Talking Points

- Start with a simple prop or room and no extra lights.
- Drag in a point light and adjust intensity and source radius.
- Replace or add a spotlight; rotate it and change cone settings.
- Show a rectangle light as a soft panel or window-like source.
- Add or adjust directional light and skylight for outdoor or environmental light.
- Compare mood words: harsh, soft, warm, cold, dramatic, calm.
- Model screenshot naming and reflection language.

## Likely Student Issues

- Students add multiple lights but do not change settings enough to see differences.
- Students confuse directional light and skylight.
- Students make the scene visible but not visually focused.
- Students overuse saturated colors until the scene becomes unreadable.
- Students forget to name which light type appears in each evidence screenshot.

## Intervention Ideas

- Ask students to point to the exact setting, object, or screenshot that proves the skill.
- Pair students for a 2-minute evidence check before submission.
- Have students reset to a simpler scene if visual clutter hides the target skill.
- For students who finish early, move them into the extension or studio challenge rather than unrelated features.

## Vocabulary Emphasis

- Point light: A light that emits from one point in all directions, like a bulb.
- Spotlight: A light that shines in one direction inside a cone shape.
- Rectangle light: A light that emits from a rectangular surface in one direction.
- Directional light: A light used for sun-like light from a far distance.
- Skylight: A light that captures the sky or distant scene and applies it to the world.
- Intensity: The strength or power of a light.
- Color temperature: A warm or cool color quality of light.
- Shadow: A darker area created when light is blocked.
- Mood: The feeling created by lighting, color, contrast, and focus.
- Focal point: The part of the scene the viewer should notice first.
